How to use the playbooks

Start here

A playbook is not a prediction machine. It is a filter. First identify the market context, then match the chart to one of the setups below, then wait for the trigger. If the chart does not clearly fit the playbook, do nothing.

Context first
Trigger second
Invalidation always
Repeatable only

Playbook rule

Important

You do not need ten setups. You need two or three setups you can execute with consistency. These playbooks are designed to give structure, not to make you overtrade. Pick the ones that match your session, personality, and risk tolerance.

Momentum setup
Opening Range Breakout
Target: 2R+
Best used when the session opens with expansion pressure and price is clearly compressing beneath or above a key intraday level.
Market conditionTrending day or strong session open with volatility expansion.
Entry triggerBreak and hold outside the opening range with momentum or reclaim after a sweep.
Stop logicBelow the opposite side of the range or beneath the breakout failure point.
Target logicNext liquidity pool, prior session level, or fixed 2R objective.
Best time: session open
Avoid: low volatility
Style: aggressive momentum
Continuation setup
Trend Pullback Entry
Target: 1.5R–3R
A clean continuation setup where price returns into value before the trend resumes. Usually cleaner than chasing breakouts.
Market conditionEstablished trend with clear higher highs/higher lows or lower highs/lower lows.
Entry triggerRejection from prior structure, reclaim of a level, or break of minor counter-trend structure.
Stop logicBeyond the pullback failure point or structural invalidation.
Target logicTrend continuation into prior high/low extension or next liquidity zone.
Best time: after first impulse
Avoid: chop
Style: patient
Reversal setup
Liquidity Sweep Reversal
Target: 2R+
Price runs obvious highs or lows, takes stops, then quickly rejects and reclaims. This setup works when the sweep changes short-term control.
Market conditionObvious equal highs/lows, range extremes, or crowded liquidity areas.
Entry triggerFast rejection candle, reclaim of the swept level, or confirmation shift in micro structure.
Stop logicBeyond the sweep extreme.
Target logicReturn to mid-range, opposite side of range, or nearest structural objective.
Best time: around key levels
Avoid: true breakout conditions
Style: tactical reversal
Trap setup
Failed Breakout
Target: 1.5R–2.5R
A breakout appears strong, attracts late traders, then fails and rotates back through the level. This is powerful when the market is actually ranging.
Market conditionRange environment, low commitment beyond key level, poor follow-through after breakout.
Entry triggerBreakout fails, price re-enters the range, and structure confirms rejection.
Stop logicBeyond the failed breakout high or low.
Target logicMiddle of range first, opposite side if momentum continues.
Best time: range markets
Avoid: trend day
Style: fade/trap
Expansion setup
Range Expansion Break
Target: 2R+
After a period of compression, price leaves the range with intent. This works when volatility is shifting from contraction to expansion.
Market conditionTight range, repeated tests, building pressure, obvious breakout boundary.
Entry triggerStrong candle close outside the range or retest-and-go pattern.
Stop logicBack inside the broken range or beneath the retest low/high.
Target logicMeasured move, next liquidity zone, or structural projection.
Best time: after compression
Avoid: fakeouts without volume
Style: breakout follow-through
Session setup
Session Momentum Shift
Target: 1R–2R
Useful when one session sets the range and the next session re-prices aggressively through it. Often strongest around London or New York transition.
Market conditionPrevious session leaves clear highs/lows and current session shows directional intent.
Entry triggerSweep of session range followed by reclaim and continuation, or clean break with acceptance.
Stop logicBeyond the shift level or session sweep extreme.
Target logicOpposite side liquidity, session extension, or daily range completion.
Best time: session change
Avoid: midday drift
Style: session-based
How to choose your best playbook Start by journaling which setups appear most often during your preferred trading hours. Then choose the two setups that feel the clearest, not the most exciting. A narrower playbook usually produces better discipline, cleaner statistics, and faster skill growth.

Common playbook mistakes

Avoid these
Forcing the match
Trying to label every chart as a setup when context is unclear.
Ignoring market condition
Taking breakout setups in range conditions or reversal setups on trend days.
Skipping confirmation
Entering just because the setup “looks close” before the trigger appears.
Changing rules live
Adjusting stop and target logic based on hope instead of the playbook.

Chart-driven playbooks

Premium format

These examples now use a full-width trading playbook layout instead of small gallery cards. This makes each setup feel more premium, more structured, and much easier for students to actually study.

MT1 • Breakout model

Breakout from Consolidation

One of the easiest high-quality setups to recognize. Price coils into a tight range, liquidity builds, and the move expands once the range finally breaks with commitment.

Teach students to focus on the base, the breakout candle, and the first logical expansion target.

Explanation

This playbook works because compression creates a visible imbalance waiting to release. Once the range breaks, the expansion often becomes the cleanest part of the move.

Entry

Enter on the breakout close or after the first retest if the level continues to hold.

Stop

Place the stop beneath the consolidation range or beneath the specific failure point that would invalidate acceptance above the level.

Targets

Take profit into the next liquidity pocket, prior swing, or measured move projection.

Risk / Reward • 1:2 to 1:4
Best environment • expansion after compression
Main mistake • chasing after extension
MT2 • Continuation model

Flag Pattern Continuation

A clean impulse move pauses into a controlled pullback and then resolves in the original direction. This is one of the best setups in the whole course for teaching patience and timing.

The quality of the setup comes from the impulse before the flag, not just from the breakout itself.

Explanation

The impulse proves strength, the flag shows temporary pause, and the breakout confirms that the original order flow is resuming.

Entry

Enter on the flag break or after a tight retest once the structure confirms continuation.

Stop

Place the stop beyond the flag low or beyond the structural point that would invalidate continuation.

Targets

Use the next expansion objective, previous trend extension, or a measured move based on the original impulse.

Risk / Reward • 1:3 to 1:5
Best environment • clean trend continuation
Main mistake • forcing flags without impulse
MT3 • Execution model

Two-Target Breakdown

This playbook combines entry quality with trade management. It teaches students how to secure profits at target one while still leaving room for a larger move into target two.

A very strong chart for teaching scale-out execution instead of all-in, all-out decision making.

Explanation

Once the move confirms, the trade can be split into a shorter objective and a runner. This improves discipline because profits are handled systematically.

Entry

Enter only after the breakdown is accepted and the first target still offers enough room relative to the stop.

Stop

Keep the stop above the breakdown failure point so risk remains tight and logical.

Targets

Target one secures the trade. Target two captures the larger move into the next clear downside objective.

Risk / Reward • 1:2 first target, 1:4+ runner
Best environment • directional follow-through
Main mistake • taking full profit too soon
