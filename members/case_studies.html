How to use case studies

Study correctly

Do not just look at the outcome. Start with context, then identify the setup, then read the trigger and invalidation. The goal is to understand why the trade made sense before you see whether it won or lost.

Context first
Setup second
Trigger third
Result last

Important rule

Professional mindset

A good case study can still show a losing trade. The job of the page is not to pretend every trade wins. The job is to show clean logic, disciplined execution, and the lesson that can be repeated next time.

Case study 01
Opening Range Breakout on Session Expansion
Result: +2.1R
A momentum example where the market opened with pressure, compressed near a key level, then expanded with acceptance.
ContextVolatility expanded at the session open and price held near the top of the opening range instead of fading.
SetupOpening Range Breakout from a compressed intraday structure.
Entry triggerStrong break and hold above the range with immediate continuation.
Stop logicBelow the breakout failure point and back inside the opening range.
Target logicNext liquidity area and fixed 2R objective.
Main lessonBreakouts work best when price has room to move and does not immediately reject the level.
Playbook: ORB
Bias: Long
Management: partial + runner
Execution grade: A
Case study 02
Trend Pullback into Prior Structure
Result: +1.8R
A continuation setup where price retraced into value, held structure, and resumed the dominant directional move.
ContextThe market was already printing continuation swings and the pullback happened into a meaningful structural level.
SetupTrend Pullback Entry aligned with the larger directional bias.
Entry triggerRejection from the zone followed by break of minor counter-trend structure.
Stop logicBeyond the pullback low that would invalidate continuation.
Target logicRetest of the prior high and extension toward the next liquidity pocket.
Main lessonPullbacks often offer cleaner entries than chasing momentum after the move has already expanded.
Playbook: Trend Pullback
Bias: Long
Management: hold to target
Execution grade: A-
Case study 03
Liquidity Sweep Reversal at a Range Extreme
Result: +2.4R
Price swept an obvious extreme, took stops, then quickly reclaimed the level and rotated back into the range.
ContextEqual highs/lows or obvious range boundaries created a visible liquidity pool.
SetupLiquidity Sweep Reversal after a stop run beyond the extreme.
Entry triggerFast reclaim of the swept level with confirmation shift in short-term structure.
Stop logicBeyond the sweep wick or the rejection extreme.
Target logicReturn to the middle of the range first, then rotation to the opposite side.
Main lessonNot every break is true acceptance. Sometimes the best opportunity is the failure after the sweep.
Playbook: Sweep Reversal
Bias: Counter-move
Management: partial at mid-range
Execution grade: B+
Case study 04
Execution Example: Management from Entry to Exit
Result: +1.3R
This study focuses less on the setup itself and more on in-trade management: partials, stop discipline, and staying aligned with the plan.
ContextThe idea was valid, but the key question was whether management would protect the edge.
SetupConfirmed entry aligned with structure and target logic.
Entry triggerExecuted at the planned confirmation point without chasing.
Stop logicStructural invalidation remained untouched until the trade advanced.
Target logicPartial was taken at the first objective and the remainder was managed according to plan.
Main lessonEven a modest win becomes valuable when execution is clean and repeatable.
Focus: Execution
Bias: Planned
Management: scale-out
Execution grade: A
What makes these studies valuable Each case study follows the same structure: context, setup, trigger, stop, target, and lesson. That consistency helps students compare trades, review execution quality, and learn how to document ideas with more clarity.

What to notice when reviewing

Study checklist
Context alignmentDid the setup match the actual market condition?
Trigger qualityWas there real confirmation or only anticipation?
Risk clarityWas invalidation clean and realistic?
Lesson valueWhat can be repeated next time, win or lose?
